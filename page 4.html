Grouping-Stereotypes

January 23,2025,2:29 PM

I find the whole groupness and stereotypes quite fascinating but also concerning. The bidirectional relationship between these two concepts has an incredibly significant implication as to how society perceives and treats those with mental health diagnoses. The notion that stereotypes only make sense when applied to a group of people is frightening. The idea of groupness adds another layer to this problem, by lumping all people with mental illness into one group, irrespective of the different mental illnesses they have, and behavioral differences we unintentionally reinforce the stereotypes associated with them. The vicious cycle makes it difficult to see mental diagnoses beyond their diagnosis. There are also types of stigmas of mental illness. Ben-Zeev, et al said,

Psychiatric disorders can have devastating impacts on people's lives due to the significant distress and disability they cause. Many advocates and scientists believe that stigmas surrounding mental illness also wreak havoc on the lives of those affected. There are three key ways in which the stigma of mental illness Can impact people’s lives. There is the phenomenon of “Public Stigma”- the negative attitudes, stereotypes, and discriminatory behaviors exhibited by the general public towards those with mental health diagnoses. The next one is “Self Stigma” - the internalization of the negative public attitude by the individual with the diagnosis, and lastly the stigma of mental illness can result in “Label Avoidance”- the tendency of some individuals to avoid disclosing their condition or seeking treatment to avoid being labeled as mentally ill. (Ben-Zeev, Dror, et al. 1).

The impacts any form of stigma has on patients are deeply troubling. When society views mental illness negatively, this public stigma leads to discrimination in many areas. With self-individual internalizes these societal biases, and with label avoidance, individuals with mental illness diagnosis have a fear of being tagged as "Mentally ill” and it can discourage them from seeking help leading to more profound consequences. Misconceptions about Mental Illness

January 23,2025,2:37 PM

Separating mentally diagnosed people from everyone else is something we do unconsciously in this society. This phenomenon extends beyond segregation, as the stigmatized individual and those labeled as “unhealthy others” find themselves ostracized within the same social group that perpetuates their marginalization. In an article by the Journal of Mental Health titled “A Mental Health Service Users Perspective to Stigmatisation”, the authors Green, Gill, et al, spoke about this very issue;

The stigmatized individual and those seen as "unhealthy others" are within the same social group that rejects their stigma. They internalize societal norms and standards, which, as observed in a study of people with HIV (Green and So bored, 200), leads to self-discrediting. Disapproval towards the stigmatized is not solely from the "normal" members; it can foster self-hatred and shame. Negative cultural attitudes contribute to internalized stigma, shaping the individual's self-concept in a self-fulfilling manner. (6)

When we talk about the 'unhealthy others,' we are referring to people who are marginalized because they do not fit into what society deems as normal. These individuals are part of the very society that marginalizes them. They grow up in it, live in it, and are exposed to its norms and expectations. To break this cycle, we need to shift societal attitudes towards acceptance and understanding, replacing judgment and prejudice with empathy and respect.

I have a sub-question to ask, how widespread is stigma? Well, the question was asked by the authors Green, Gill, et al, and they went on to answer that question by saying “Many studies have shown that socially stigmatizing attitudes towards people with mental health problems are widespread (Byrne, 1997, 2001). In a recent survey in Britain (Crisp et al., 2000), people with a range of seven mental disorders were perceived by the general population as hard to talk to, ‘different’, and unpredictable. People with schizophrenia, alcoholism, and drug addiction were thought by 70% of those surveyed to be dangerous, and substance misusers were thought to be blameworthy for their condition. Differentness, unpredictability, dangerousness, and blame have all been identified as factors that fuel stigma (Jones et al., 1984).” (12).

The widespread perception of those with mental illness as ‘different', 'unpredictable’, and even ‘dangerous’ irrespective of the difference in every mental issue is indicative of the prejudices within our Society.
